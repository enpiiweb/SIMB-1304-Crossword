Do I detect a note of resentment by Margaret Dennis towards the ACC’s super solvers? In any year there are at least 78 prizes which solvers are eligible to win without having to compete in the cumulatives. The prizes for these puzzles are now more than substantial, $75 for correctly solving just one puzzle, whereas in the most recent cumulative slots 1 to 5 contest the prize for each of the ‘super solvers’ was only $116 for correctly solving 30 puzzles!

We should be encouraging all members to perform consistently well throughout all the published ACC puzzles and the cumulative prizes should reflect reward for the super efforts required in attaining perfection.

In any normal tournament most super performers are rewarded very well for their efforts. In the most recent slot 6 competition, each of the 18 super solvers received only $17. I doubt that these cumulative prizes in comparison to the non-cumulative prizes are sufficient to adequately encourage and reward members. Margaret’s disdain for those super solvers who consistently win appears to be a case of ‘sour grapes’!

I support June Parker’s sentiment in selecting slot 1 to 5 prize-winners at random, but in any one year, once a member has won one of these prizes, then others should be given a chance. This then rewards the maximum number of members with a monthly slot 1 to 5 prize. —Alan Walter

I would like to comment on Margaret Dennis's thoughts on the prize procedure. Good on you Margaret for sharing your thoughts with us all. I feel that, in most endeavours, top achievers are rewarded - we can all endeavour to reach the top, but for myself the challenge of competing is most important. My congratulations and admiration for those who can so successfully do what I try really hard to do!!! You deserve your prizes. Like Alan Walter I think the 1 to 5 arrangement of random draw from correct entries is the only way to go, but I also think that members should be eligible for the prize for each 1 to 5 puzzle only once in each year. Alan Walter asked for the meaning of "Cook" in my acrostic puzzle dealing with Magnetic Island. Here is my reply: As a child living in Townsville, I was always told that the iron in the island made Cook's compass veer from true north and so he named the island Magnetic Island. -Audrey Ryan
